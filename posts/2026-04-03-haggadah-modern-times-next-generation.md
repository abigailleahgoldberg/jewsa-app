# The Haggadah in Modern Times: Teaching the Exodus Story to a New Generation

There is no single Haggadah. There are thousands. In every generation and every community, Jews have retold the Passover story in the language and context of their time. Medieval mystics wrote Haggadot laced with Kabbalah. The Maxwell House Haggadah — published since 1932 — has been used at more American Seders than any other single text in history.

This multiplicity is not a sign of confusion. It is the genius of the tradition. The question for every family gathering around the Seder table this Passover is: what does this generation need from this story?

## Why the Seder Was Designed for the Present

"In every generation, each person is obligated to see themselves as if they personally left Egypt." This is not metaphor. It is methodological instruction. The Seder's entire structure is designed to create not intellectual understanding but experiential identification.

You don't learn about the Exodus at the Seder. You participate in it. The bitter herbs sting your eyes. The salt water is in your mouth. The matzah — this bread of affliction — is the same bread your ancestors ate in haste as they left.

Every generation finds its own Egypt — its own form of constriction and oppression — and its own path toward liberation.

## Choosing (or Building) Your Haggadah

- **Maxwell House Haggadah** — The old reliable. Functional, familiar, takes about 45 minutes.
- **The New American Haggadah** (Jonathan Safran Foer, ed.) — A literary reimagining with new translations and contemporary commentary.
- **A Different Night** (Noam Zion & David Dishon) — The best Haggadah for a creative, discussion-focused Seder.
- **My Jewish Learning's Free Haggadah** — Available online, customizable, practical.

Or: build your own. Many families maintain a family Haggadah that accumulates annotations and traditions over decades.

## Making the Seder Work for Multiple Generations

**For young children:** Give them specific roles — pouring Elijah's cup, asking the questions, hiding the afikomen. Use props. Engagement beats instruction every time.

**For teenagers:** Connect the story to their world. Where do you see people enslaved today — economically, politically, systemically? What does it mean to be free? Who is still waiting for liberation?

**For skeptics and secular family members:** The Haggadah offers an entirely secular reading. The Exodus narrative as the foundational text of human liberation movements doesn't require theological belief to find meaningful.

**For non-Jewish guests:** They are explicitly welcome. "Whoever is hungry, let them come and eat." Brief explanations of the plate's symbolism go a long way.

## Songs: Don't Skip Them

Dayenu alone is worth the entire Seder. Chad Gadya is a minor musical masterpiece. Echad Mi Yodea becomes progressively more frantic and ridiculous. These songs are memory-making. They're the Seder your grandchildren will remember when you're gone.

## The Obligation to Tell the Story

The Hebrew word for Egypt — Mitzrayim — means "narrow place," "constriction." The Exodus was a movement from narrowness to expansiveness. Every generation encounters its narrow places. Every generation has to decide whether to stay in familiar constriction or make the terrifying, uncertain journey toward freedom.

The Seder doesn't just tell the story. It invites you to place yourself in it.

This year at your Seder table, find the narrow place. Find the liberation. Tell the story as if it happened to you — because in the ways that matter most, it did.

Chag Sameach.
