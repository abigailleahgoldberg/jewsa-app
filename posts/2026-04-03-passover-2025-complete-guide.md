# Passover 2026: The Complete Guide to Seder Traditions, Symbols, and Meaning

Passover — Pesach in Hebrew — begins at sundown on April 5, 2026 and continues through April 13. It is one of the most widely observed Jewish holidays, celebrated by millions of Jewish families across the world, and one of the most rich in symbolism, tradition, and communal meaning.

At its heart, Passover is the story of liberation. The Exodus from Egypt — from slavery to freedom — is not just history in the Jewish tradition. It is a living story that every generation is commanded to retell as if they themselves were among those who left Egypt.

## What Is Passover?

The Passover story is told in the Book of Exodus. The Israelites, enslaved in Egypt for generations, cried out to God. Moses was called to lead them out. The final plague — the death of the firstborn — "passed over" the homes of the Israelites who had marked their doorposts with lamb's blood, giving the holiday its name. The Israelites left Egypt in such haste that they had no time to wait for their bread to rise — giving us matzah, the unleavened bread central to all eight days of Passover.

## The Seder: Structure and Meaning

The Seder — Hebrew for "order" — is the ceremonial meal that begins Passover. It follows the Haggadah through 15 steps and includes these key elements:

**The Seder Plate (Ke'arah):**
- **Maror (Bitter Herbs)** — horseradish or romaine, representing the bitterness of slavery
- **Charoset** — nuts, fruit, wine, spices; symbolizing the mortar enslaved Israelites used to build Egyptian monuments
- **Karpas** — parsley or celery dipped in salt water; the salt water represents tears; karpas represents spring renewal
- **Zeroa** — roasted lamb bone or chicken bone, representing the Passover sacrifice
- **Beitzah** — roasted egg, symbolizing the festival sacrifice and the cycle of life
- **Chazeret** — additional bitter herbs for the Hillel sandwich

**The Four Cups of Wine** correspond to God's four promises of redemption in Exodus 6:6-7. Grape juice is a fully acceptable substitute.

**The Four Questions (Mah Nishtanah)** — traditionally asked by the youngest child, asking why this night is different from all others. The entire Seder is structured to answer.

**Elijah's Cup** — a fifth cup poured for the prophet Elijah, whose arrival heralds the messianic era. The door is opened during the Seder to welcome him.

**The Afikomen** — the middle matzah is broken and hidden; children traditionally "steal" it and must be persuaded to return it. The Seder cannot end until the afikomen is eaten.

## Passover Preparation: Removing Chametz

In the weeks before Passover, observant families remove all chametz — any food made from wheat, barley, rye, oats, or spelt that has been allowed to ferment. In the mystical tradition, chametz represents ego and spiritual obstruction. The night before Passover, a final search by candlelight (bedikat chametz) is performed; any chametz found is burned the following morning.

## Traditional Passover Foods

- **Matzah ball soup** — the great debate: light and fluffy vs. dense "sinkers"
- **Brisket** — slow-cooked, deeply savory centerpiece
- **Tzimmes** — sweet stew of carrots, sweet potatoes, and dried fruit
- **Flourless chocolate cake** — the beloved Passover dessert
- **Matzah brei** — fried matzah, breakfast of the holiday week

## The Universal Message

"Whoever is hungry, let them come and eat. Whoever is in need, let them come and celebrate Passover with us." The Passover story — oppression, liberation, covenant — resonates far beyond the Jewish community. The Exodus narrative gave language to liberation movements across centuries, from the American founding to the civil rights movement to anti-colonial struggles worldwide.

This Passover, at your Seder table, that weight is present in every cup of wine, every bite of bitter herb, every piece of matzah. The story is ancient. The obligation is now.

Chag Pesach Sameach — a joyful and meaningful Passover to all who celebrate.
